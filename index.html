JARGON DECONSTRUCTED

Vasanth Kumar

In economics, the word ‘capital’ means different things depending on the context. Since this can be puzzling, I’ve made an attempt to demystify the word by providing a general overview.

  1. Capital as a Factor of Production: The tangible and intangible resources that are used in the production of goods and services such as machines, tools, technology refer to capital as a factor of production.

  1. Physical Capital: Human-made goods that are used for producing goods. e.g., buildings, machines, etc. These are sometimes also called fixed capital, as they are long-term assets.
  2. Intangible capital: includes patents and copyrights etc.

Capital-intensive refers to the process of production that relies more on capital goods (goods that are used to produce other goods eg. machines) than labour.

  1. Financial Capital : Financial capital is the money that is used to purchase things that are required for production.
  1. Working Capital: refers to the short-term funds involved in meeting the day-to-day expenses of production.
  2. Equity Capital: Funds provided by shareholders (e.g., share capital).  
  3. Debt Capital: Borrowed funds (e.g., loans, bonds).  
  4. It may also be used for the unused money that the firm has or any funds that the firm can use for investment.

‘Capital’  in  capital flight (sudden outflow of capital during times of uncertainty) refers to this type of capital.

3. Capital as an Asset:

While this also comes under capital goods which are part of factors of production, capital assets also refer to valuable long term holdings such as securities and real estate that may not be directly tied to production. Here, capital refers to wealth — real estate, jewellery, shares, stocks, bonds etc. Capital assets sometimes generate returns eg., rental property and stocks.

 4. Capital Market:

Capital Market is a financial market to trade long-term financial instruments such as stocks and bonds. The share market is part of the capital market.

5. Capital in Government Accounts: Government budget is divided into Receipts (income) and expenditure which is further subdivided into revenue receipts/expenditure and capital receipts/expenditure. Revenue receipts refer to the tax and non-tax revenue that the government gets such as GST and fines. Whereas,

  1. Capital receipts refer to the money that the government received through borrowings, loans, sale of public assets(disinvestment) and returns from capital assets that the government owns. Unlike revenue receipts, capital receipts are non-recurring which means capital receipts are not a regular source of income. Capital receipts often come at the cost of creating liabilities(loans) or liquidating assets.

  1. Capital Expenditure is the money that the government spends on loan payments, investments, infrastructure, development of machinery, equipment, building, health facilities, education, etc.

 6. Capital in Balance of Payments (BoP):  

The current account records exports and imports in goods, trade in services and transfer payments. The capital account records all international purchases and sales of assets such as money, stocks, bonds, etc. It also includes foreign investments and loans.

7. Miscellaneous:

  1. Capital accumulation: long term process of accumulating stock of capital after depreciation. Here capital means capital goods as well as wealth.
  2. Human capital: Technical know-how, expertise and skills.
  3. In other fields, such as sociology, social capital refers to the resources we get from our social network and relationships, while cultural capital refers to our knowledge, tastes and skills that we get from our upbringing and education.
  4. In political economy, financial capital has different meanings going way beyond the mainstream conception.  

To conclude, capital is largely connected with concepts like wealth and resources that are often used for or ultimately aid in production. Further, the lines are sometimes blurred since all these types are sometimes closely related. Moreover, depending on what school of economic thought a person subscribes to, they will come up with different classifications.
